1. The Molecular Scrollyteller: The Gleevac

The Synthesis Puzzle

The chemical formula for Imanitib (brand name Gleevac) is C29H31N7O. It is used for treatment of chronic myelogenous leukemia and gastrointestinal stromal tumours. It has a role as an antineoplastic agent, a tyrosine kinase inhibitor and an apoptosis inducer.

Following its launch in 2001, Novartis aggressively protected its patent and continually raised the drug's price in the US market. In a landmark 2013 ruling, the Indian Supreme Court denied Novartis a renewed patent for the drug, preventing the "evergreening" of its exclusivity. This historic decision allowed Indian pharmaceutical companies to continue synthesizing generic versions of the life-saving molecule for a fraction of the global price.

The ICER Formula

The Incremental Cost-Effectiveness Ratio (ICER) evaluates the economic value of an intervention:

ICER = (Δ Cost) / (Δ Effect)
Effect is measured in QALYs

By dropping the cost of the intervention while maintaining the exact same clinical effect (the molecule is identical), the generic alternative maximizes public health utility per dollar spent.

Kaplan-Meier Survival Data

Prior to Imatinib, the 5-year survival rate for CML was ~30%. With Imatinib, survival skyrocketed to nearly 90%, yielding roughly 5 additional QALYs per patient over baseline.

The Policy Math & Economic Theory
1. The Epidemic Model of Diffusion
dnt = β (nt / N) (N - nt) dt
  • Where nt is the number of adopters and N is the total population.
  • Because knowledge spreads like a contagion, the solution to this differential equation yields a logistic, S-shaped curve.
2. Complementary Assets
  • Diffusion requires "complementary assets": organizational change, infrastructure, and capabilities.
  • For Indian generics, low drug prices were not enough. The S-curve only triggered rapid acceleration once global NGOs (like PEPFAR) built the cold-chain distribution networks necessary to deliver the drugs across Sub-Saharan Africa.

1. The Lerner Index of Monopoly Power
L = (P - MC) / P

The Lerner Index (L) measures a firm's pricing power. P is Market Price, MC is Marginal Cost.

  • Patented L: (5000 - 20) / 5000 = 0.996 (Extreme Monopoly)
  • Generic L: (105 - 20) / 105 = 0.810 (Competitive Margin)

↳ Source: Lerner, A. P. (1934). "The Concept of Monopoly and the Measurement of Monopoly Power." The Review of Economic Studies, 1(3), 157–175.

2. WHO / HAI Affordability Index
A = C / W

Where C is the cost of a standard treatment course, and W is the daily wage of the lowest-paid unskilled government worker.

The Policy Math & Economic Theory
1. The Herfindahl-Hirschman Index
H = Σ Si2
  • The index calculates concentration by adding up the squares of every firm's market share percentage.
  • Squaring the shares severely penalizes markets dominated by a single giant.
  • < 1,500: Unconcentrated
  • 1,500 - 2,500: Moderate
  • > 2,500: Monopoly Threat
2. The Innovation Debate
  • Arrow (1962) proposed that monopolists lack the drive to innovate because new products simply cannibalize their existing sales (the replacement effect).
  • Schumpeter (1942) countered that only massive monopolies possess the financial capital and economies of scale required to survive high-risk R&D failures.
3. The Inverted-U Relationship
  • Aghion et al. (2005) merged these theories into an inverted-U model.
  • In moderately competitive markets, closely matched firms invest heavily in R&D to break ahead of the pack.
  • In hyper-competitive markets, prices are driven down so close to marginal cost that no firm retains enough profit to fund research.

↳ Source: Aghion, P., et al. (2005). "Competition and Innovation: an Inverted-U Relationship." The Quarterly Journal of Economics, 120(2), 701–728.

9. Game Theory: The "Winner-Takes-All" Patent Race

Why do pharmaceutical companies spend billions on duplicative research rather than sharing the burden? Patents operate as a "winner-take-all" contest. The first firm to secure the patent captures total market control, while the loser gets absolutely nothing and absorbs the entire cost of their failed investment. Test this dynamic below.

The Policy Math & Economic Theory
1. The R&D Investment Rule
p × V ≥ C
  • Investment is rational if the probability of winning (p) multiplied by the patent value (V) is greater than the development costs (C).
  • If spending is unequal, the high-spender secures the patent outright, meaning their probability is 1.
  • If spending is identical, the odds become a coin toss (p = 0.5) for both firms.
2. The Dominant Strategy
  • Calculating the expected net payoffs mathematically: P/2 - Ch vs -Cl.
  • Playing High R&D yields a better financial outcome regardless of the competitor's choice.
  • In game theory, a move that is always superior regardless of the opponent's action is called a dominant strategy.
3. Socially Wasteful Outcomes
  • If both firms agreed to stick to Low R&D, they would both net $400M instead of $100M.
  • However, the winner-take-all threat of losing the patent entirely forces both to overspend.
  • This creates hyper-competition resulting in excessive, overlapping research that drains resources from a societal perspective.
